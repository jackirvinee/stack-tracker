# ULTIMATE ADHD SUPPLEMENT STACK — Evidence-Based Research Report

## Based on PubMed Literature Review (44 searches, ~100 papers reviewed)

**Date:** March 19, 2026
**Medications:** Wellbutrin XL (bupropion), Dexedrine XR and IR (dextroamphetamine)
**Goal:** Maximize therapeutic efficacy, minimize tolerance development, protect against stimulant-induced neuronal damage, and support cardiovascular health — all grounded in peer-reviewed evidence from PubMed.

## 1. CURRENT STACK ANALYSIS

### L-Tyrosine 500mg — Wake, Empty Stomach
**Grade: B+ (Keep, timing correct, dose adequate for daily use)**

L-Tyrosine is the direct precursor to L-DOPA via tyrosine hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme in dopamine synthesis. This enzyme requires iron, vitamin B6, and copper as cofactors — meaning tyrosine supplementation only works if those cofactors are present. Twelve PubMed results confirm tyrosine's role in preventing cognitive decline under dopamine-depleting stress conditions.

- **Dose note:** Acute stress studies use 100-150 mg/kg (7-10g for a 70kg person), but chronic daily supplementation at 500-2000mg is standard practice. The current 500mg dose is at the lower end but reasonable for daily use alongside stimulants that are already increasing dopamine turnover.
- **Timing is correct:** Must be taken on an empty stomach because tyrosine competes with other large neutral amino acids (from dietary protein) for transport across the blood-brain barrier.
- **Cofactor dependency:** Without adequate iron (ferritin), B6, and copper, supplemental tyrosine cannot be efficiently converted to dopamine. The B50 complex at breakfast covers B6. Iron status should be verified via blood work.

### B50 Complex — Breakfast
**Grade: A- (Keep)**

B vitamins serve multiple critical roles in this stack. 139 PubMed results address B vitamins, homocysteine, and cognitive function.

- **B6 (pyridoxine/P5P):** Cofactor for both tyrosine hydroxylase (tyrosine to L-DOPA) and aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase/DOPA decarboxylase (L-DOPA to dopamine). Directly supports dopamine synthesis.
- **B12 and folate:** Lower homocysteine, which is neurotoxic at elevated levels and associated with cognitive decline.
- **B3 (niacin/nicotinamide):** Tung et al. (2017) demonstrated that nicotinamide blocked amphetamine-induced energy failure — one of the three steps in the neurotoxicity cascade (energy failure, then excitotoxicity, then free radical formation). [DOI: 10.1016/j.neulet.2017.01.002](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neulet.2017.01.002)
- **Timing:** With breakfast and fat is appropriate for absorption of fat-soluble forms.

### Electrolyte Capsules (2) — Breakfast
**Grade: A (Keep, may need to increase)**

Dextroamphetamine is a sympathomimetic that increases sweating, suppresses appetite (reducing electrolyte intake from food), and can cause dry mouth — all of which accelerate electrolyte depletion. Deng et al. (2021) documented significant weight and BMI reductions with stimulant use, especially in the first 6 months. [DOI: 10.1016/j.pbb.2021.173225](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pbb.2021.173225). Nanda et al. (2023) confirmed decreased appetite as the most common side effect. [DOI: 10.7759/cureus.45995](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.45995)

- **Consideration:** If experiencing muscle cramps, brain fog unrelated to medication timing, or afternoon fatigue, add a second dose of electrolytes in the afternoon.

### Vitamin D3 2500 IU — Breakfast with Fat
**Grade: A (Keep)**

Cass et al. (2023) found that calcitriol (active vitamin D) protected against methamphetamine-induced serotonin depletion in animal models, suggesting a neuroprotective role for vitamin D in the context of amphetamine use. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neuint.2023.105590](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuint.2023.105590)

- **Dose:** 2500 IU is reasonable for maintenance. Optimal serum 25(OH)D target is 40-60 ng/mL. Test levels to confirm adequacy; some individuals require 4000-5000 IU.
- **Timing:** Correct — D3 is fat-soluble and should be taken with the fattiest meal.

### Krill Oil (2 softgels) — Breakfast with Fat
**Grade: B- (Keep but likely underdosed — action required)**

The omega-3 evidence for ADHD is nuanced:

- Liu et al. (2023) meta-analysis found omega-3 did NOT significantly improve ADHD symptoms overall, BUT supplementation lasting 4 or more months DID show significant benefit (SMD -0.35). [DOI: 10.4088/JCP.22r14772](https://doi.org/10.4088/JCP.22r14772)
- Zhou et al. (2024) network meta-analysis ranked omega-3 among the top interventions for improving attention on parent rating scales. [DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0296926](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0296926)
- Vergara et al. (2025) review found an EPA:DHA ratio of 2:1 or greater is most effective for psychiatric conditions, with 1-2g/day EPA recommended. [DOI: 10.1093/nutrit/nuaf201](https://doi.org/10.1093/nutrit/nuaf201)

**The krill oil bioavailability advantage is real but the dose problem is critical:**

- Sung et al. (2018) demonstrated krill oil EPA was approximately 30% more bioavailable per mg than fish oil due to the phospholipid-bound form. [DOI: 10.6133/apjcn.092017.03](https://doi.org/10.6133/apjcn.092017.03)
- Guarneiri et al. (2023) confirmed phospholipid-enhanced fish oil had similar total absorption to krill oil but higher peak concentration. [DOI: 10.1016/j.nut.2023.112090](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.nut.2023.112090)
- **PROBLEM:** Most krill oil capsules deliver only 100-150mg EPA+DHA per capsule. Two capsules yield approximately 200-300mg total omega-3. Even with the 30% bioavailability advantage, this is far below the 1-2g EPA target. You would need 4-6 krill oil capsules daily, OR switch to a high-EPA concentrated fish oil (e.g., 1g EPA per softgel).
- **ACTION:** Check your krill oil label for EPA and DHA content per capsule. Calculate whether you are reaching at least 1g EPA daily (accounting for ~30% bioavailability bonus). If not, either increase krill oil capsules or add a high-EPA fish oil supplement.

### Magtein (MgL-Threonate) 667mg AM + 1334mg PM
**Grade: A+ (Cornerstone supplement — keep, dosing is excellent)**

This is arguably the most important supplement in the stack. The evidence converges from multiple directions:

**Tolerance prevention via NMDA antagonism:**
- Wolf (1998) — this landmark review established that NMDA receptor antagonists prevent the development of behavioral sensitization to amphetamine. This is the core mechanism by which tolerance develops: repeated amphetamine exposure causes NMDA-dependent neuroplastic changes in the mesolimbic dopamine system. Blocking NMDA receptors during amphetamine exposure prevents these changes. [DOI: 10.1016/s0301-0082(97)00090-7](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0301-0082(97)00090-7)
- Ohmori et al. (1994) confirmed that both competitive and noncompetitive NMDA antagonists block sensitization to methamphetamine. [DOI: 10.1016/0091-3057(94)90318-2](https://doi.org/10.1016/0091-3057(94)90318-2)
- Magnesium is a natural, voltage-dependent NMDA receptor channel blocker. At resting membrane potential, Mg2+ ions physically occlude the NMDA receptor ion channel. This makes magnesium supplementation a physiological approach to NMDA antagonism without the side effects of pharmaceutical NMDA blockers like memantine or MK-801.
- Magtein specifically: a 2026 RCT demonstrated that 6 weeks of Magtein supplementation improved overall cognition, produced a 7.5-year brain age reduction, improved working memory and reaction time, and also reduced resting heart rate and improved heart rate variability. [DOI: 10.3389/fnut.2025.1729164](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnut.2025.1729164)

**Neuroprotection:**
- Tung et al. (2017) showed the amphetamine neurotoxicity cascade proceeds: energy failure, then NMDA-mediated excitotoxicity, then free radical formation. NMDA antagonism (which magnesium provides) directly blocks step 2 of this cascade. MK-801 (an NMDA antagonist) blocked free radical formation in their model. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neulet.2017.01.002](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neulet.2017.01.002)

**Dosing:** The current split dose of 667mg AM / 1334mg PM (total 2001mg Magtein, delivering approximately 144mg elemental Mg) is well-structured. The higher PM dose supports sleep and maximizes NMDA blockade during the overnight recovery period.

### Mag Bisglycinate Powder 200-300mg Elemental — Before Bed
**Grade: A (Keep)**

Complementary to Magtein. Glycinate form has excellent bioavailability, promotes relaxation via glycine co-delivery, and adds substantial elemental magnesium to ensure total daily intake reaches 400-600mg elemental. This addresses both the NMDA antagonism goal and general magnesium adequacy (most adults are deficient).

### ALCAR 1500mg — Afternoon
**Grade: C+ (Weak ADHD evidence — consider repurposing the slot)**

- Sarris et al. (2011) systematic review found "mixed (mainly inconclusive)" evidence for ALCAR in ADHD. [DOI: 10.1016/j.ctim.2011.06.007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ctim.2011.06.007)
- The WFSBP/CANMAT 2022 guidelines gave acetyl-L-carnitine a "-" (NOT recommended) rating for ADHD. [DOI: 10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041](https://doi.org/10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041)
- **However:** ALCAR does support mitochondrial function, fatty acid transport into mitochondria, and acetylcholine production. Given that Tung et al. (2017) identified energy failure as the first step in amphetamine neurotoxicity, mitochondrial support has theoretical value even without direct ADHD symptom evidence.
- **Verdict:** Not harmful, potentially neuroprotective via mitochondrial support, but the ADHD-specific evidence does not justify prioritizing it. Consider whether this slot would be better filled by creatine (stronger evidence for brain energy) or CoQ10 (cardiovascular protection). If budget/pill burden is not an issue, keeping ALCAR alongside adding creatine is reasonable.

### NAC 600mg — End of Stim Window
**Grade: A (Keep — timing is strategic and correct)**

NAC is doing triple duty in this stack:

1. **Glutathione restoration:** Dean et al. (2011) demonstrated that NAC restores brain glutathione levels after d-amphetamine exposure. Amphetamine depletes brain glutathione (17 PubMed studies confirm this pattern), and glutathione is the brain's primary endogenous antioxidant. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neulet.2011.05.027](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neulet.2011.05.027)

2. **Does NOT blunt therapeutic effects:** Fort et al. (2024) showed that NAC did NOT attenuate cue-induced amphetamine seeking — meaning it provides neuroprotection without interfering with the desired therapeutic action of the stimulant. [DOI: 10.1016/j.bbr.2024.115261](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbr.2024.115261)

3. **Glutamate modulation:** NAC modulates the cystine-glutamate antiporter, normalizing extracellular glutamate levels. Cuocina et al. (2024) meta-analysis found NAC reduced craving in substance use disorders (SMD -0.61), relevant to preventing compulsive re-dosing patterns. [DOI: 10.3389/fphar.2024.1462612](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphar.2024.1462612)

4. **Cognitive protection from NMDA disruption:** Phensy et al. (2017) found NAC prevented cognitive and social behavioral deficits caused by NMDA receptor blockade, via restoring oxidative balance. [DOI: 10.3389/fnbeh.2017.00106](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnbeh.2017.00106)

5. **Direct neuroprotection:** Capela et al. (2007) showed NAC reduced MDMA neurotoxicity by restoring glutathione levels. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neuro.2007.04.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuro.2007.04.005)

- **Dose:** 600mg is the low end. Most studies showing benefit use 1200-2400mg/day. Consider increasing to 1200mg (600mg x 2) — one at end of stim window, one before bed. NAC has a short half-life (~6 hours), so split dosing improves coverage.
- **Timing:** End of stim window is strategic. The oxidative stress from amphetamine peaks during and immediately after the period of highest dopamine release. Deploying NAC as the stimulant wears off captures the peak oxidative window.

### Vitamin C 500-1000mg — End of Stim Window
**Grade: A (Keep — timing is exactly right)**

Vitamin C serves a dual and seemingly contradictory role, but the timing resolves the paradox:

**Urinary acidification (clearance acceleration):**
- Dextroamphetamine is a weak base (pKa ~10). Its renal clearance is pH-dependent: acidic urine dramatically increases excretion, while alkaline urine prolongs it. Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) acidifies urine, increasing amphetamine clearance.
- **Taking it at end of stim window uses this as a feature, not a bug.** It accelerates clearance of residual dextroamphetamine before bed, improving sleep onset and reducing cardiovascular stress during sleep.

**Neuroprotection (when separated from Dex):**
- Consoli et al. (2020) demonstrated that ascorbate deficiency decreases dopamine release, establishing vitamin C as neuroprotective for the dopaminergic system. [DOI: 10.1111/jnc.15151](https://doi.org/10.1111/jnc.15151)

**CRITICAL TIMING RULE:** Never take vitamin C within 2 hours of dextroamphetamine dosing. It will prematurely clear the medication and reduce therapeutic efficacy. The end-of-stim-window timing in the current stack is correct and intentional.

## 2. CRITICAL TIMING RULES

These rules are pharmacologically non-negotiable:

### Rule 1: Vitamin C Must Be Separated from Dextroamphetamine by 2+ Hours
Ascorbic acid acidifies urine and accelerates amphetamine renal clearance. Taking vitamin C near Dex dosing will reduce medication effectiveness. End-of-stim-window timing exploits this for faster evening clearance.

### Rule 2: L-Tyrosine Must Be Taken on an Empty Stomach
Tyrosine competes with other large neutral amino acids (phenylalanine, tryptophan, leucine, isoleucine, valine) for the LAT1 transporter across the blood-brain barrier. Taking it with protein-containing food dramatically reduces brain uptake. Minimum 30 minutes before eating; 60 minutes is ideal.

### Rule 3: Iron (If Added) Must Be Separated from Everything
Iron competes with zinc, calcium, and magnesium for absorption. It also binds to tannins (tea/coffee) and phytates. Take iron on an empty stomach with vitamin C (but not at the same time as Dex — use a morning dose of vitamin C specifically for iron absorption if needed, well before the first Dex dose). Separate from magnesium, zinc, and calcium by at least 2 hours.

### Rule 4: Zinc and Magnesium Compete for Absorption
If taking both, separate by at least 1-2 hours, or take zinc with a meal and magnesium separately. The current stack takes magnesium at breakfast and bedtime — zinc (if added) should go with lunch or dinner.

### Rule 5: NAC on Empty or Near-Empty Stomach
NAC absorption is reduced by food. The end-of-stim-window timing works well because appetite suppression from Dex means the stomach is likely empty. If adding a second NAC dose at bedtime, take it at least 30 minutes after any evening snack.

### Rule 6: Fat-Soluble Vitamins with Fat
Vitamin D3, vitamin E, vitamin K2, and omega-3s all require dietary fat for absorption. The breakfast-with-fat timing for D3 and krill oil is correct. CoQ10 (if added) is also fat-soluble.

### Rule 7: Creatine Timing Does Not Matter
Creatine monohydrate can be taken at any time of day, with or without food. Consistent daily intake is what matters, not timing.

## 3. NEUROPROTECTION PROTOCOL

**This is the most important section.** Therapeutic doses of amphetamine are not equivalent to neurotoxic doses used in animal studies, but chronic daily use over years warrants proactive neuroprotection. The mechanisms are well-characterized in PubMed.

### The Amphetamine Neurotoxicity Cascade (Tung et al., 2017)

Tung et al. (2017) established the three-step cascade through which amphetamine damages dopaminergic neurons:

**Step 1: Energy Failure** — Amphetamine increases metabolic demand in dopaminergic neurons beyond mitochondrial capacity.
- **Blocked by:** Nicotinamide (vitamin B3), creatine (brain energy buffer), ALCAR/CoQ10 (mitochondrial support)

**Step 2: NMDA-Mediated Excitotoxicity** — Energy failure leads to loss of the voltage-dependent Mg2+ block on NMDA receptors, causing excessive calcium influx.
- **Blocked by:** Magnesium (restores the NMDA channel block), memantine (pharmaceutical NMDA antagonist)

**Step 3: Free Radical Formation** — Calcium overload triggers reactive oxygen species production, overwhelming endogenous antioxidant defenses.
- **Blocked by:** NAC/glutathione, vitamin C, vitamin E, selenium, CoQ10

[DOI: 10.1016/j.neulet.2017.01.002](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neulet.2017.01.002)

### Antioxidant Status in Stimulant Users

Walker et al. (2014) found that stimulant-dependent patients had **45% lower total antioxidant capacity** and **29% lower superoxide dismutase (SOD)** activity compared to controls. This confirms that chronic stimulant use depletes antioxidant reserves. [DOI: 10.1002/hup.2430](https://doi.org/10.1002/hup.2430)

### Your Current Stack's Neuroprotective Coverage

| Neurotoxicity Step | Mechanism | Current Protection | Gap? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Step 1: Energy Failure | Mitochondrial overload | B3 (in B50), ALCAR | Add creatine |
| Step 2: Excitotoxicity | NMDA calcium influx | Magtein + Mg bisglycinate | Well covered |
| Step 3: Free Radicals | ROS overwhelm antioxidants | NAC, Vitamin C | Add vitamin E, selenium, CoQ10 |

### Specific Neuroprotective Evidence by Supplement

**Selenium:**
- Sanchez et al. (2003): Selenium deficiency doubled MDMA neurotoxicity in animal models. Selenium-replete animals showed 50% less dopamine depletion. [DOI: 10.1016/s0028-3908(02)00411-2](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0028-3908(02)00411-2)
- **Implication:** Selenium adequacy may halve stimulant-induced dopamine system damage. 200mcg/day selenomethionine is the standard safe dose.

**Vitamin E:**
- Roghani et al. (2001): Vitamin E reduced dopaminergic neuron loss by 65% in a 6-OHDA model. [DOI: 10.1016/s0006-8993(00)03296-0](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0006-8993(00)03296-0)
- **Implication:** As a fat-soluble antioxidant, vitamin E protects neuronal membranes where lipid peroxidation from ROS occurs. 200-400 IU mixed tocopherols/day.

**Vitamin D:**
- Cass et al. (2023): Calcitriol protected against methamphetamine-induced serotonin depletion. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neuint.2023.105590](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuint.2023.105590)
- Already in stack at 2500 IU. Verify serum levels are 40-60 ng/mL.

**NAC:**
- Dean et al. (2011): Restores brain glutathione after amphetamine. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neulet.2011.05.027](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neulet.2011.05.027)
- Capela et al. (2007): Reduced MDMA neurotoxicity via glutathione restoration. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neuro.2007.04.005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuro.2007.04.005)
- Already in stack. Consider dose increase to 1200mg/day.

**Magnesium:**
- Wolf (1998): NMDA antagonism prevents amphetamine sensitization/tolerance. [DOI: 10.1016/s0301-0082(97)00090-7](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0301-0082(97)00090-7)
- Already in stack at therapeutic doses. This is the backbone of the neuroprotection protocol.

## 4. SUPPLEMENTS TO ADD — STRONG EVIDENCE

### 1. Zinc — 30mg/day (15mg BID)
**Evidence Strength: A (RCT with dose-response)**

Arnold et al. (2011) conducted an RCT and found that **30mg/day zinc resulted in a 37% lower optimal amphetamine dose** in children with ADHD. Critically, 15mg/day was NOT sufficient — only the 30mg/day group showed the dose-reduction effect. The mechanism is likely through zinc's role as a modulator of dopamine transporter function and its involvement in over 300 enzymatic processes including those in dopamine metabolism. [DOI: 10.1089/cap.2010.0073](https://doi.org/10.1089/cap.2010.0073)

The WFSBP/CANMAT 2022 guidelines gave zinc a "+/-" (equivocal) rating for ADHD monotherapy, but the amphetamine dose-reduction finding is distinct from monotherapy efficacy. [DOI: 10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041](https://doi.org/10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041)

- **Dose:** 30mg elemental zinc per day, divided 15mg twice daily
- **Form:** Zinc picolinate or zinc bisglycinate for best absorption
- **Timing:** With meals (lunch and dinner) to avoid nausea. Separate from magnesium and iron by 2 hours.
- **Duration:** Ongoing. Recheck zinc and copper levels periodically — chronic zinc supplementation can deplete copper.
- **Copper balance:** Add 1-2mg copper daily if supplementing zinc long-term, unless getting adequate copper from diet (shellfish, liver, dark chocolate, nuts).

### 2. Creatine Monohydrate — 3-5g/day
**Evidence Strength: A- (strong cognitive evidence, strong mechanistic rationale for neuroprotection)**

Korovljev et al. (2025) RCT demonstrated that creatine supplementation increased frontal brain creatine levels, improved reaction time, and modulated lipid profiles. [DOI: 10.1080/27697061.2025.2551184](https://doi.org/10.1080/27697061.2025.2551184)

79 total PubMed results exist for creatine and cognitive function. Creatine serves as a phosphocreatine energy buffer in the brain, rapidly regenerating ATP during periods of high metabolic demand. This is directly relevant because Tung et al. (2017) identified energy failure as the initiating event in amphetamine neurotoxicity.

- **Dose:** 3-5g creatine monohydrate daily. No loading phase needed.
- **Timing:** Any time of day, with or without food. Add to a morning shake or take with breakfast.
- **Form:** Creatine monohydrate (Creapure brand for purity). Creatine HCl is also effective per Korovljev et al. but monohydrate has the deepest evidence base and is the cheapest supplement on this entire list.
- **Note:** May cause minor water retention initially. Stay well-hydrated (which you should already be doing on stimulants).

### 3. Selenium — 200mcg/day
**Evidence Strength: B+ (compelling neuroprotective evidence)**

Sanchez et al. (2003): Selenium deficiency doubled MDMA neurotoxicity; selenium-replete animals had 50% less dopamine depletion. [DOI: 10.1016/s0028-3908(02)00411-2](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0028-3908(02)00411-2)

Selenium is a required cofactor for glutathione peroxidase, the enzyme that uses glutathione to neutralize reactive oxygen species. Without adequate selenium, even if NAC restores glutathione levels, the glutathione cannot be effectively utilized.

- **Dose:** 200mcg selenomethionine daily (do not exceed 400mcg — toxicity risk)
- **Timing:** With breakfast
- **Form:** Selenomethionine or selenium-enriched yeast
- **Note:** Brazil nuts contain highly variable selenium content (10-800mcg per nut depending on soil). Supplementation provides more reliable dosing.

### 4. CoQ10 (Ubiquinol) — 100-200mg/day
**Evidence Strength: B+ (cardiovascular protection + mitochondrial support)**

Dual benefit for this stack:

**Cardiovascular protection:** A 2025 meta-analysis of 45 RCTs found CoQ10 reduces systolic blood pressure by 3.44 mmHg, with best results at doses under 200mg/day for more than 8 weeks. [DOI: 10.1016/j.ijcrp.2025.200424](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ijcrp.2025.200424)

This is directly relevant because Chan et al. (2025) Cochrane review of 56 RCTs found amphetamines cause sustained increases: SBP +1.93 mmHg, DBP +1.84 mmHg, HR +3.71 bpm. [DOI: 10.1002/14651858.CD007896.pub4](https://doi.org/10.1002/14651858.CD007896.pub4)

**Mitochondrial support:** CoQ10 is an essential component of the electron transport chain. It addresses Step 1 (energy failure) of the Tung et al. neurotoxicity cascade.

- **Dose:** 100-200mg daily
- **Form:** Ubiquinol (reduced form, better absorption than ubiquinone)
- **Timing:** With breakfast (fat-soluble, needs dietary fat)

### 5. Vitamin E — 200-400 IU/day (mixed tocopherols)
**Evidence Strength: B+ (neuroprotective)**

Roghani et al. (2001): Reduced dopaminergic neuron loss by 65%. [DOI: 10.1016/s0006-8993(00)03296-0](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0006-8993(00)03296-0)

Vitamin E is the primary fat-soluble antioxidant protecting neuronal cell membranes against lipid peroxidation — the final common pathway of free radical damage in Step 3 of the neurotoxicity cascade.

- **Dose:** 200-400 IU daily
- **Form:** Mixed tocopherols (d-alpha, d-beta, d-gamma, d-delta tocopherol) — NOT synthetic dl-alpha-tocopherol
- **Timing:** With breakfast (fat-soluble)
- **Caution:** Do not exceed 400 IU daily. High-dose vitamin E (>400 IU) has been associated with increased all-cause mortality in some meta-analyses.

## 5. SUPPLEMENTS TO ADD — MODERATE EVIDENCE

### 6. Taurine — 1-3g/day
**Evidence Strength: B (cardiovascular, moderate neuroprotective)**

Taurine reduces blood pressure, prevents endothelial dysfunction, and restores nitric oxide bioavailability. [DOI: 10.1371/journal.pone.0105851](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0105851)

Given the sustained cardiovascular burden from chronic dextroamphetamine use (Chan et al., 2025), taurine adds a distinct cardiovascular protective mechanism beyond CoQ10 — it works on endothelial function and NO bioavailability rather than mitochondrial electron transport.

- **Dose:** 1-3g daily
- **Timing:** Can be split AM/PM or taken as a single dose. Often included in electrolyte formulations.
- **Bonus:** Taurine is a mild GABA-A agonist, which may help with stimulant-related anxiety without causing sedation.

### 7. Phosphatidylserine (PS) — 200mg/day
**Evidence Strength: B (meta-analytic support, but a safety signal exists)**

Bruton et al. (2021) meta-analysis found 200-300mg/day PS significantly improved inattention (effect size 0.36). [DOI: 10.1089/acm.2020.0432](https://doi.org/10.1089/acm.2020.0432)

Hirayama et al. (2013) RCT showed 200mg PS improved ADHD symptoms and short-term auditory memory in children. [DOI: 10.1111/jhn.12090](https://doi.org/10.1111/jhn.12090)

**CAUTION — SAFETY SIGNAL:** Subasi et al. (2025) published a case report where phosphatidylserine combined with citicoline caused suicidality in a child on methylphenidate. While this is a single case report and involved citicoline (not part of this stack) and methylphenidate (not dextroamphetamine), it warrants caution when combining PS with any stimulant medication. [DOI: 10.5152/pcp.2025.251257](https://doi.org/10.5152/pcp.2025.251257)

- **Dose:** 200mg daily (start low, do not combine with citicoline or alpha-GPC)
- **Timing:** With breakfast
- **Monitoring:** Watch for mood changes, particularly in the first 2-4 weeks. Discontinue if any mood worsening occurs.
- **Verdict:** The meta-analytic evidence is solid, but introduce this one cautiously and in isolation (not simultaneously with other new supplements) so any adverse effects can be attributed.

### 8. Melatonin — 0.3-1mg, 2-3 Hours Before Bed
**Evidence Strength: B (for sleep in ADHD/stimulant users)**

17 PubMed results address melatonin with ADHD, stimulants, and sleep. Stimulant-related sleep disruption is extremely common.

Avery (1998) noted that low doses are often more effective, and supraphysiological doses may be counterproductive — high doses can cause morning grogginess, paradoxical wakefulness, and desensitize melatonin receptors. [DOI: 10.3109/07853899808999394](https://doi.org/10.3109/07853899808999394)

- **Dose:** 0.3-1mg (NOT 3-5mg). Start with 0.3mg.
- **Timing:** 2-3 hours before target sleep time (NOT 30 minutes before bed). This targets the circadian phase-advance effect rather than the acute sedative effect.
- **Form:** Immediate-release tablets or liquid for precise low dosing
- **Note:** This complements the vitamin C end-of-stim-window strategy. Vitamin C accelerates Dex clearance; melatonin 2-3 hours before bed shifts the circadian clock forward. Together they create a two-pronged approach to stimulant-related sleep disruption.

### 9. Probiotics
**Evidence Strength: B- (for general brain health; ADHD-specific evidence is mixed)**

42 PubMed results address the gut-brain axis, ADHD, and the microbiome. The WFSBP/CANMAT 2022 guidelines gave probiotics a "++" (provisionally recommended) rating for depression as adjunct therapy, though ADHD-specific evidence is weaker. [DOI: 10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041](https://doi.org/10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041)

Abhishek et al. (2024) review found mixed results for probiotics in ADHD specifically. [DOI: 10.7759/cureus.69804](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.69804)

- **Rationale for inclusion:** Stimulant-induced appetite suppression alters eating patterns, which affects the microbiome. Wellbutrin can cause GI side effects. General brain health benefits may support the stack's goals even without direct ADHD symptom improvement.
- **Dose:** Multi-strain probiotic containing Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium species, 10-50 billion CFU
- **Timing:** Morning on empty stomach, or with breakfast

- Sarris et al. (2011): "mixed (mainly inconclusive)" evidence. [DOI: 10.1016/j.ctim.2011.06.007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ctim.2011.06.007)
- WFSBP/CANMAT 2022: NOT recommended (-) for ADHD. [DOI: 10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041](https://doi.org/10.1080/15622975.2021.2013041)
- **Not harmful** and has theoretical mitochondrial support value, but should not be relied upon as an ADHD intervention. If keeping it, reframe its purpose as mitochondrial neuroprotection rather than ADHD symptom management. If needing to reduce pill burden, this is the most justifiable cut.

### Lion's Mane for ADHD
- Chong et al. (2019) review found it promotes NGF expression and has potential antidepressant effects, but there are zero ADHD-specific clinical trials. [DOI: 10.3390/ijms21010163](https://doi.org/10.3390/ijms21010163)
- **Verdict:** Interesting nootropic, insufficient evidence to include in an evidence-based ADHD stack. May have value for Wellbutrin users seeking additional antidepressant support, but that is speculative.

### Iron — Only If Deficient (Do NOT Supplement Empirically)
- Calarge et al. (2010): Serum ferritin inversely correlated with ADHD severity AND with optimal amphetamine dose. 23% of ADHD children had ferritin below 7 ng/mL. [DOI: 10.1089/cap.2010.0053](https://doi.org/10.1089/cap.2010.0053)
- Araujo et al. (2026) review: Evidence is inconsistent, but iron is a cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase (the enzyme converting L-tyrosine to L-DOPA). [DOI: 10.7759/cureus.103196](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.103196)
- Shvarzman et al. (2022): Reduced brain iron in ADHD was localized to the limbic striatum and correlated with symptom severity. [DOI: 10.1007/s00221-022-06484-7](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00221-022-06484-7)
- **CRITICAL:** Iron overload is dangerous — it causes oxidative stress (the opposite of what this stack aims to prevent), organ damage, and is a risk factor for neurodegenerative disease. Get ferritin tested FIRST. Only supplement if ferritin is below 30-50 ng/mL. Target ferritin of 50-100 ng/mL. If supplementing, 18-36mg elemental iron (ferrous bisglycinate for tolerability) on an empty stomach with vitamin C.

## 7. NUTRIENT MANAGEMENT FOR HEAVY DEXEDRINE USE

### The Appetite Suppression Problem

Deng et al. (2021) documented significant weight and BMI Z-score reductions with stimulant use, especially in the first 6 months of treatment. [DOI: 10.1016/j.pbb.2021.173225](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pbb.2021.173225)

Nanda et al. (2023) confirmed that decreased appetite is the single most common side effect of stimulant medications. [DOI: 10.7759/cureus.45995](https://doi.org/10.7759/cureus.45995)

### Strategies

**1. Front-load calories before Dex kicks in:**
- Eat a substantial breakfast 30-45 minutes before Dex XR onset (or take Dex immediately after eating)
- Include protein (eggs, Greek yogurt) to provide amino acid substrate for neurotransmitter synthesis
- Include fat for fat-soluble supplement absorption (D3, krill oil, CoQ10, vitamin E)

**2. Calorie-dense, low-volume foods during the stim window:**
- Nut butters, avocado, olive oil, cheese, trail mix
- Protein shakes/smoothies (liquid calories are easier to consume with suppressed appetite)
- Add creatine to shakes (tasteless, dissolves)

**3. Protein prioritization:**
- Minimum 1.2-1.6g protein per kg bodyweight daily
- Protein provides tyrosine, phenylalanine, and tryptophan — precursors for dopamine and serotonin
- If appetite suppression makes this difficult, use whey/casein/plant protein powder in shakes

**4. Post-stim recovery meal:**
- As Dex wears off (coinciding with end-of-stim-window supplements), appetite typically returns
- Use this window for the largest meal of the day
- Include protein, complex carbohydrates, and healthy fats

**5. Electrolyte management:**
- Stimulants increase sympathetic tone, causing sweating and dry mouth
- Current 2 caps at breakfast may be insufficient depending on activity level and climate
- Signs of electrolyte deficiency: muscle cramps, headaches, brain fog, irregular heartbeat
- Consider adding afternoon electrolyte dose or sipping electrolyte water throughout the day

**6. Hydration:**
- Amphetamines are dehydrating
- Minimum 2.5-3L water daily
- Coffee/tea count but also contribute to dehydration — compensate 1:1

## 9. BLOOD WORK TO GET

Request these tests from your prescribing physician. Many are not part of standard panels and must be specifically ordered.

### Priority 1 — Get These First

| Test | Target Range | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Ferritin** | 50-100 ng/mL | Iron status for tyrosine hydroxylase function. Calarge 2010: inversely correlated with ADHD severity and amphetamine dose. Only supplement if low. |
| **Serum Zinc** | 80-120 mcg/dL | Baseline before starting zinc. Arnold 2011: 30mg/day reduced optimal Dex dose by 37%. |
| **25(OH) Vitamin D** | 40-60 ng/mL | Ensure D3 dose is adequate. Cass 2023: neuroprotective. |
| **RBC Magnesium** | 5.0-6.5 mg/dL | More accurate than serum Mg. Confirms Magtein/bisglycinate are achieving tissue saturation. |
| **Complete Blood Count (CBC)** | Normal ranges | Baseline health, rule out anemia. |
| **Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP)** | Normal ranges | Liver/kidney function baseline. Relevant because this is a heavy supplement stack. |

### Priority 2 — Get Within 3 Months

| Test | Target Range | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Homocysteine** | <10 umol/L (ideal <8) | B vitamin adequacy marker. Elevated homocysteine is neurotoxic. |
| **Copper (serum)** | 70-150 mcg/dL | Monitor if supplementing zinc long-term (zinc depletes copper). |
| **hs-CRP** | <1.0 mg/L | Systemic inflammation marker. Baseline for monitoring. |
| **Lipid Panel** | Standard targets | Cardiovascular baseline given chronic stimulant use. |
| **Fasting Insulin** | <7 uIU/mL | Metabolic health marker. |
| **Selenium (serum or RBC)** | 100-150 ng/mL | Confirm adequacy before relying on it for neuroprotection. |

### Priority 3 — Annual Monitoring

| Test | Target Range | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Thyroid Panel (TSH, free T3, free T4)** | TSH 0.5-2.5; normal T3/T4 | Wellbutrin and stimulants can affect thyroid. L-tyrosine is a thyroid precursor. |
| **Cortisol (AM)** | 10-20 mcg/dL | Chronic stimulant use and HPA axis. |
| **B12 and Folate** | B12 >500 pg/mL; folate >15 ng/mL | Confirm B50 is maintaining levels. |
| **GGT** | <30 U/L | Sensitive liver marker for supplement load. |
| **Blood Pressure + Resting HR** | <130/85; HR <85 | Chan 2025 Cochrane: amphetamines cause sustained BP/HR increases. Track trends. |

## 11. ADVANCED COMPOUNDS — PEPTIDES AND RESEARCH CHEMICALS

**Evidence Level: Preclinical (animal studies) for most. No human ADHD trials. Use with extreme caution.**

These compounds are available from research chemical suppliers online. They are NOT FDA-approved supplements. They represent the frontier of neuroprotection and cognitive enhancement research. According to PubMed:

### Tier 1: Strongest Neuroprotection Evidence for Stimulant Users

**BPC-157 (Body Protection Compound)**
- Gastric pentadecapeptide that directly counteracted amphetamine-induced vascular failure, brain swelling, hemorrhage, and organ damage in rats. [DOI: 10.3390/ph16060788](https://doi.org/10.3390/ph16060788)
- Counteracted dopamine system disturbances including sensitization from chronic methamphetamine, resolved NMDA receptor interactions. [DOI: 10.4103/1673-5374.320969](https://doi.org/10.4103/1673-5374.320969)
- Counteracted L-NAME-induced catalepsy and schizophrenia-like symptoms from amphetamine/apomorphine/MK-801. [DOI: 10.1016/j.bbr.2020.112919](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.bbr.2020.112919)
- **Route:** Oral (stable in gastric acid) or subcutaneous. 250-500mcg 1-2x/day typical research dose.
- **Relevance to Dex users:** The most directly relevant peptide — studied specifically with amphetamine and dopamine system modulation.

**Semax (ACTH 4-7 analogue)**
- Synthetic heptapeptide with neuroprotective and nootropic properties. Normalized monoamine levels after SSRI disruption. [DOI: 10.1016/j.npep.2020.102114](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.npep.2020.102114)
- Targets mu-opioid receptor to regulate deubiquitination, reduces oxidative stress, promotes functional recovery after spinal cord injury. [DOI: 10.1111/bph.70122](https://doi.org/10.1111/bph.70122)
- Approved in Russia as a nasal spray for cognitive enhancement and stroke recovery.
- **Route:** Intranasal. 200-600mcg/day typical research dose.
- **Relevance:** Broad neuroprotection, monoamine normalization — could theoretically support dopamine system health during chronic stimulant use.

### Tier 2: Moderate Evidence, Neuroprotective/Cognitive

**Selank (Tuftsin analogue)**
- Protected against ethanol-induced memory impairment by regulating BDNF in hippocampus and prefrontal cortex. [DOI: 10.1007/s10517-019-04588-9](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10517-019-04588-9)
- Anxiolytic and cognitive-enhancing. Approved in Russia alongside Semax.
- **Route:** Intranasal. 250-500mcg/day.
- **Relevance:** BDNF support + anxiety reduction could complement stimulant therapy.

**Noopept (GVS-111)**
- Dipeptide that increases NGF and BDNF expression in hippocampus. Neuroprotective against oxidative stress models. [DOI: 10.1016/j.lfs.2019.116698](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lfs.2019.116698)
- Reduced cognitive deficits in Alzheimer's model, decreased malondialdehyde (lipid peroxidation marker). PMID: 20184279
- **Route:** Oral or sublingual. 10-30mg/day.
- **Relevance:** NGF/BDNF upregulation could support neuroplasticity during chronic stimulant use.

**Methylene Blue (pharmaceutical grade)**
- Mitochondrial electron carrier that enhances Complex IV function. Intranasal MB showed neuroprotection: rescued neuron loss, synaptic proteins, reduced apoptosis, improved mitochondrial morphology. [DOI: 10.3389/fnbeh.2025.1648837](https://doi.org/10.3389/fnbeh.2025.1648837)
- 9 PubMed results for methylene blue + mitochondria + neuroprotection + cognition.
- **Route:** Oral (USP grade only). 0.5-4mg/kg. Very low doses (0.5-1mg/kg).
- **Relevance:** Directly addresses the energy failure step in Tung 2017's amphetamine neurotoxicity cascade.

### Tier 3: Experimental / Limited Evidence

**NSI-189**
- Neurogenic molecule that promotes hippocampal neurogenesis, increases hippocampal volume, enhances synaptic plasticity. In clinical trials for MDD. [DOI: 10.2337/db19-0271](https://doi.org/10.2337/db19-0271)
- Enhanced LTP and reversed impairments in motor and cognitive functions. [DOI: 10.1016/j.neuropharm.2018.10.038](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropharm.2018.10.038)
- **Route:** Oral. 40mg/day in clinical trials.
- **Relevance:** Hippocampal neurogenesis could theoretically counteract any long-term stimulant effects on brain structure.

**Dihexa (PNB-0408)**
- Angiotensin IV analogue with synaptogenic properties. Showed promise for AD/PD via HGF/c-Met receptor system. [DOI: 10.1016/j.pneurobio.2014.11.004](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pneurobio.2014.11.004)
- However, FAILED to protect in Huntington's disease model. [DOI: 10.3233/JHD-231507](https://doi.org/10.3233/JHD-231507)
- **Route:** Oral or intranasal. Extremely potent — low mcg doses.
- **Caution:** Potent growth factor stimulation raises theoretical cancer risk. Very limited safety data.

### Critical Warnings for Peptide/Research Chemical Use
1. **Quality control is the #1 risk.** Research chemical vendors are unregulated. Purity, contamination, and mislabeling are common. According to PubMed, seized preparations of Semax and Selank from online vendors were confirmed to contain the stated peptides, but this cannot be assumed. [DOI: 10.1002/dta.2717](https://doi.org/10.1002/dta.2717)
2. **No human clinical trials exist** for most of these in the context of ADHD stimulant neuroprotection.
3. **Drug interactions** with Wellbutrin and Dexedrine are completely unstudied for most peptides.
4. **BPC-157 has the strongest case** given direct amphetamine studies, but all data is preclinical (rat).
5. These are NOT first-line interventions. Optimize the supplement stack in Sections 1-8 first.

## DISCLAIMER

**This document is a research compilation derived from peer-reviewed literature indexed in PubMed. It is NOT medical advice.** The studies cited include animal models, in vitro data, and clinical trials of varying quality — not all findings translate directly to clinical practice.

**Before making ANY changes to your supplement regimen:**
1. Discuss this report with your prescribing physician and/or a clinical pharmacist
2. Get baseline blood work (Section 9) to guide decisions
3. Introduce new supplements ONE AT A TIME, with 1-2 weeks between additions, to identify any adverse reactions or interactions
4. Monitor blood pressure and heart rate regularly given chronic stimulant use
5. Report any adverse effects to your prescribing physician immediately

**Specific warnings:**
- Wellbutrin XL lowers the seizure threshold. Some supplements (e.g., high-dose B6) may theoretically interact. Discuss with your physician.
- Dextroamphetamine is a scheduled substance with cardiovascular effects. The cardiovascular protection supplements (CoQ10, taurine) do NOT replace medical monitoring.
- Iron supplementation without confirmed deficiency can cause oxidative damage — the exact opposite of this stack's neuroprotective goals. Test first.
- The phosphatidylserine safety signal (Subasi 2025) warrants caution. Introduce carefully and monitor mood.
- Many neuroprotection studies used supraphysiological amphetamine doses in animal models. Therapeutic ADHD doses carry lower risk, but the precautionary principle supports proactive protection for chronic daily use spanning years or decades.
